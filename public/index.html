    Core Structure
    "Research is formalized curiosity. It is poking and prying with a purpose." — Zora Neale Hurston
    1Identify a Research Gap

    Every great paper begins with a gap — something the literature doesn't explain, a contradiction to resolve, or a new angle on an old problem.

    • Read 20–30 recent papers to map the current landscape
    • Look for "future work," "limitations," or "open questions" in conclusions
    • Define your research question in one precise sentence before proceeding
    2Systematic Literature Review

    A literature review is a critical synthesis that situates your work. It is not a summary.

    • Use Google Scholar, Semantic Scholar, PubMed, IEEE Xplore, JSTOR
    • Search with keywords + Boolean operators (AND, OR, NOT)
    • Use Zotero (free) for citation management
    • Group papers thematically — theories, methods, findings, debates
    3Design Your Methodology
    • Quantitative: experiments, surveys, statistical analysis — for measuring and generalizing
    • Qualitative: interviews, case studies, ethnography — for depth and meaning
    • Mixed methods: combines both for triangulation
    • Address validity, reliability, and ethics
    4Collect Data & Analyze
    • Keep a detailed research journal — every decision matters in peer review
    • Use reproducible, version-controlled workflows
    • Apply appropriate statistical tests; use thematic analysis for qualitative data
    • Negative results are still publishable

    📄 The IMRaD Structure

    Title & Abstract
    Hook + research question + method + key finding + significance. Abstract ≤ 250 words; must stand alone.
    Introduction
    Broad context → specific problem → gap → solution → paper outline. Classic funnel shape.
    Literature Review
    Critical synthesis, organized thematically. End by showing your gap and how you fill it.
    Methodology
    Design, participants/data, instruments, procedure, analysis. Detailed enough for full replication.
    Results
    Objective findings only — data, tables, figures. No interpretation here. Let numbers speak first.
    Discussion
    Interpret results, compare with literature, explain surprises, acknowledge limits, discuss implications.
    Conclusion
    Summarize contributions, answer your research question, suggest future work. No new information.
    References
    Every in-text citation must appear here. Use one consistent style: APA 7, IEEE, Chicago, AMA, MLA.
    5Academic Writing Principles
    • Write results first, then introduction — frame what you already know
    • Active voice: "We analyzed…" not "It was analyzed…"
    • One idea per paragraph; lead with the topic sentence
    • Every claim needs data or a citation
    6Choosing a Journal & Submitting
    • Use Scimago (SJR) or Journal Citation Reports (JCR) to evaluate journals
    • Match scope, methodology, and audience to the journal
    • Check impact factor, quartile (Q1–Q4), and acceptance rate
    • Follow formatting guidelines exactly — rejections happen over formatting
    7Peer Review
    • Outcomes: Accept / Minor Revision / Major Revision / Reject
    • Respond to each reviewer comment specifically and respectfully
    • Top journals reject 80–95% of submissions — resubmit elsewhere if rejected
    8After Publication
    • Upload preprint to arXiv, SSRN, or ResearchGate for open access
    • Share on LinkedIn, Twitter/X, and academic networks
    • Track citations via Google Scholar or Scopus to build your h-index
